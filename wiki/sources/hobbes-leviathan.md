**Original file:** `raw/raw/Leviathan.pdf`

**First published:** 1651
**Type:** Philosophical Treatise

---

## Summary

*Leviathan* is Thomas Hobbes's most significant work, written during the English Civil War. It establishes the foundations of social contract theory and modern political philosophy. Hobbes argues for the necessity of a strong, central authority to avoid the chaos of the state of nature. The title refers to the biblical sea monster, used as a metaphor for the absolute power of the commonwealth.

---

## Key Arguments

1. **Materialist Psychology**: Hobbes begins with a materialist account of human nature, viewing sense, imagination, and reason as mechanical processes. He defines "good" and "evil" as simply objects of desire and aversion.

2. **The State of Nature**: Hobbes famously describes the state of nature as a "war of all against all" (*bellum omnium contra omnes*). Due to natural equality, competition, and fear (diffidence), life in this state is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

3. **Laws of Nature**: Rationality (reason) suggests "articles of peace," which Hobbes calls the Laws of Nature. The first law is to seek peace; the second is to be willing to lay down one's right to all things when others are likewise willing; the third is that men perform their covenants made.

4. **The Social Contract**: To secure peace, individuals must enter into a covenant to transfer their rights to a common power. This creates the "Leviathan" or Commonwealth—an artificial person represented by a sovereign.

5. **Absolute Sovereignty**: The sovereign's power must be absolute, indivisible, and inalienable. Hobbes argues that a divided power (such as between a king and parliament) leads to civil war. The sovereign is the ultimate judge of all doctrine and law.

6. **The Right of Nature**: Subjects retain the inalienable right to defend their own lives, even against the sovereign, because the contract was entered into specifically for the preservation of life.

7. **Religion and the State**: Hobbes argues that the sovereign must also be the head of the church to prevent religious conflict from destabilizing the commonwealth.
