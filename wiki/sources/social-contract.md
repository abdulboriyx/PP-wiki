## Summary

This SEP entry provides a comprehensive overview of contractarianism as both a political theory (legitimacy of authority) and a moral theory (origin of moral norms). It distinguishes contractarianism from contractualism, surveys historical and contemporary figures, and covers major critiques.

---

## Key Arguments

1. **The two-strain distinction**: Contractarianism (Hobbesian, self-interest-based) vs. contractualism (Kantian, reason/justifiability-based). These are often conflated but are analytically distinct.

2. **Elements of the social contract**: Every contractarian theory requires (a) a characterization of the initial situation (state of nature, original position, initial bargaining position) and (b) a characterization of the parties (rationality and motivation).

3. **Gauthier's programme**: *Morals by Agreement* attempts to ground morality in rationality via minimax relative concession; later revised toward maximin proportionate gain using Pareto-optimality rather than bargaining.

4. **Hampton's dilemma**: If cooperation failures stem from passion, parties will still be passionate after the contract; if from rationality (prisoner's dilemma), they will defect after the contract too. Either way, compliance is not secured.

5. **Subversive contractarianism**: Pateman (*The Sexual Contract*, 1989) and Mills (*The Racial Contract*, 1997) use contractarian logic to explain and condemn patriarchy and white supremacy as implicit contracts among dominant groups.

6. **Inclusion problem**: Normative contractarianism struggles to include the severely disabled, animals, and the global poor, since inclusion requires capacity for reciprocal contribution.
