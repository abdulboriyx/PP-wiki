# Argument: The Noble Lie Stabilizes the City

## Argument in One Sentence

Plato argues that the ideal city may need a founding civic myth to bind citizens together and make hierarchy appear natural.

## Source

[The Republic](../sources/plato-republic.md)  
**Author:** [Plato](../thinkers/plato.md)  
**Pages / Section:** Book III, PDF pp. 239-274

## Original Text Fragment

> "one royal lie... and at any rate the rest of the city"

## Why This Is an Argument

The noble lie is not a casual falsehood. It is an institutional device meant to solve a political problem: how to secure unity and obedience in a stratified city. The argument moves from the need for civic cohesion to the use of myth as a stabilizing instrument.

## How the Text Supports the Argument

The fragment supports the argument because it openly presents falsehood as politically useful. Plato is explicit that the myth is directed at the city and connected to rule.

## Broader Explanation

The myth of metals teaches citizens that they share a common origin while also having different natural roles. Gold souls should rule, silver souls should assist, and bronze or iron souls should produce. The myth therefore combines solidarity and hierarchy.

This creates one of the strongest tensions in *The Republic*. The city is supposedly ruled by philosophers devoted to truth, yet it may depend on a politically useful falsehood.

## Underlying Assumptions

- Civic unity requires shared symbolic belief.
- Most citizens cannot be governed by philosophical truth alone.
- Hierarchy is more stable when experienced as natural rather than imposed.

## What This Argument Does Not Prove

It does not prove that deception is morally legitimate. It also does not prove that hierarchy is natural. It shows Plato's political use of myth, not the truth of the myth.

## Opposing Views

- **Liberal public reason:** Political legitimacy should not depend on official falsehood.
- **Democratic theory:** Citizens should be treated as participants in judgment, not objects of civic mythology.
- **Critical theory:** Founding myths can mask domination and reproduce hierarchy.
