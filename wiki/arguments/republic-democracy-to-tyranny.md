# Argument: Democracy Tends Toward Tyranny in The Republic

## Argument in One Sentence

Plato argues that democracy can decay into tyranny when the love of freedom becomes hostility to all rational order.

## Source

[The Republic](../sources/plato-republic.md)  
**Author:** [Plato](../thinkers/plato.md)  
**Pages / Section:** Books VIII-IX, PDF pp. 401-456

## Original Text Fragment

> "tyranny naturally arises out of democracy"

## Why This Is an Argument

Plato connects regime change to moral psychology. Democracy honors freedom and equality among ways of life; this produces a soul that treats all desires as equally authoritative. When desire becomes lawless, the democratic soul and city become vulnerable to domination by a tyrannical desire or tyrannical ruler.

## How the Text Supports the Argument

The fragment directly states Plato's transition thesis. It supports the argument because it identifies tyranny not as democracy's accidental opposite, but as its possible internal outcome.

## Broader Explanation

Books VIII-IX classify regimes and souls together. Timocracy, oligarchy, democracy, and tyranny are political forms, but they are also character types. Democracy is attractive because it is varied and free, but Plato thinks that unranked freedom dissolves discipline.

The tyrant is the political image of a soul ruled by lawless appetite. This is why Plato treats tyranny as both civic slavery and psychic misery.

## Underlying Assumptions

- Regimes express dominant forms of soul.
- Freedom without rational order becomes self-undermining.
- Political equality among desires is psychologically dangerous.

## What This Argument Does Not Prove

It does not prove that every democracy historically becomes tyranny. It does not distinguish carefully among different democratic institutions. The argument is a normative psychology of regime decay, not a modern empirical law.

## Opposing Views

- **Democratic republicanism:** Freedom can be sustained through institutions, civic virtue, and law.
- **Liberal democracy:** Rights and checks can restrain both majority passion and tyrants.
- **Tocquevillean analysis:** Democracy has dangers, but its pathologies are more complex than direct decay into tyranny.
