# Argument: Contractarianism and Contractualism Are Distinct Strains

## Argument in One Sentence

The SEP entry argues that contractarianism and contractualism should not be conflated because they ground agreement in different motivations and standards.

## Source

[Contractarianism (SEP)](../sources/social-contract.md)  
**Author:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry  
**Pages / Section:** Introduction

## Original Text Fragment

> "two distinct strains of social contract thought"

## Why This Is an Argument

The entry is making a classificatory argument. It separates Hobbesian theories based on self-interest and mutual advantage from Kantian theories based on public justifiability and respect for persons.

## How the Text Supports the Argument

The fragment supports the argument because it states the distinction the entry uses to organize the whole discussion. The rest of the source proceeds by focusing mainly on the Hobbesian contractarian strain.

## Broader Explanation

Contractarianism explains morality or authority through rational agreement among self-interested agents. Contractualism explains moral principles through what can be justified to each person. Both use contract imagery, but they answer different questions with different moral psychologies.

This distinction matters because criticisms of one strain do not always apply to the other. For example, the exclusion problem is especially sharp for mutual-advantage contractarianism, while contractualism faces different problems of justification and reasonable rejection.

## Underlying Assumptions

- The contract tradition contains importantly different families of argument.
- Motivation matters for classifying social contract theories.
- Hobbesian and Kantian approaches should be analyzed separately.

## What This Argument Does Not Prove

It does not prove that every thinker fits cleanly into one category. It also does not prove that either strain succeeds normatively.

## Opposing Views

- **Broad social contract interpretation:** Treats all contract theories as variations of one tradition.
- **Historical interpretation:** May resist imposing contemporary labels on Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant, and Rawls.
