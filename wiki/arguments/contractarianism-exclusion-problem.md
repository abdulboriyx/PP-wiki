# Argument: The Exclusion Problem for Contractarianism

## Argument in One Sentence

Normative contractarianism risks excluding those who cannot reciprocate benefits because its standard of inclusion is mutual advantage.

## Source

[Contractarianism (SEP)](../sources/social-contract.md)  
**Author:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry  
**Pages / Section:** Sections 4 and 6

## Original Text Fragment

> "beyond the scope of the rules of justice"

## Why This Is an Argument

The argument identifies a structural consequence of mutual-advantage reasoning. If parties are included because they can benefit others or threaten noncooperation, then those unable to reciprocate may not count as parties to justice.

## How the Text Supports the Argument

The fragment supports the argument because it names the exclusionary implication directly. The surrounding section applies the problem to severe disability, animals, and others whose claims cannot be grounded easily in reciprocal advantage.

## Broader Explanation

Contractarianism is powerful because it explains why self-interested agents would accept constraints. But that same strength creates a boundary problem. If justice is only rational among mutually beneficial cooperators, then people or beings who cannot contribute may receive protection only indirectly.

This connects to feminist, disability, animal ethics, and racial critiques of contract theory. It also motivates [Subversive Contractarianism](../debates/subversive-contractarianism.md), where the contract is used to expose domination rather than justify authority.

## Underlying Assumptions

- Contractarian inclusion depends on reciprocal advantage.
- Some beings cannot participate as bargaining agents.
- A theory of justice should explain duties to vulnerable non-reciprocators.

## What This Argument Does Not Prove

It does not prove that no contractarian solution is possible. Some theorists appeal to insurance, trust, indirect duties, or broader accounts of contribution. But it shows that inclusion is not automatic.

## Opposing Views

- **Becker-style contractarian inclusion:** Many disabled people and caregivers can be included for strategic reasons.
- **Trust-based contractarianism:** Vulnerability may contribute to a cooperative social climate.
- **Contractualism / capabilities approach:** Duties do not depend on reciprocal productive contribution.
