# Argument: Hampton's Compliance Dilemma

## Argument in One Sentence

Hampton argues that Hobbesian contractarianism struggles to explain why people who cannot cooperate before the contract will comply after it.

## Source

[Contractarianism (SEP)](../sources/social-contract.md)  
**Author:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry  
**Pages / Section:** Section 4, Critiques of Normative Contractarianism

## Original Text Fragment

> "will still be motivated by these passions after the social contract"

## Why This Is an Argument

The dilemma attacks the transition from state-of-nature failure to post-contract compliance. If conflict comes from passion, passion remains after contract. If conflict comes from rational defection, rational agents still have reason to defect unless something changes.

## How the Text Supports the Argument

The fragment supports one horn of the dilemma: a contract does not automatically transform motivation. The surrounding discussion adds the rationality horn, where prisoner's-dilemma reasoning also survives the contract.

## Broader Explanation

Contractarianism must explain both agreement and compliance. It is not enough to show that peace would benefit everyone. The theory must also explain why self-interested agents keep the terms when cheating is profitable.

This critique matters for Hobbes and Gauthier. Hobbes answers with sovereign enforcement; Gauthier appeals to constrained maximization and dispositions to comply. Hampton pressures both by asking whether the solution smuggles in moral motivation or relies on coercion rather than contract.

## Underlying Assumptions

- A successful contract theory must explain stable compliance.
- The causes of pre-contract conflict may persist after agreement.
- Rational agreement is not the same as reliable motivation.

## What This Argument Does Not Prove

It does not prove contractarianism is false. It identifies a serious burden: the theory must explain how motivation changes or how enforcement solves compliance.

## Opposing Views

- **Hobbes:** Coercive sovereign power changes incentives enough to secure compliance.
- **Gauthier:** Rational agents can adopt constrained maximization.
- **Contractualism:** Obligation may rest on justifiability rather than self-interest.
