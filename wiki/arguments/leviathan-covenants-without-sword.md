# Argument: Covenants Need Coercive Enforcement

## Argument in One Sentence

Hobbes argues that agreements cannot secure peace unless backed by a coercive power strong enough to enforce them.

## Source

[Leviathan](../sources/hobbes-leviathan.md)  
**Author:** [Thomas Hobbes](../thinkers/hobbes.md)  
**Pages / Section:** Part II, Chapters XVII-XVIII, PDF around pp. 98-111

## Original Text Fragment

> "laws are of no power to protect them without a sword"

## Why This Is an Argument

Hobbes moves from agreement to enforcement. People may see that peace is rational, but fear of others' noncompliance undermines trust. A covenant without coercive enforcement leaves each person exposed to the same insecurity the covenant was meant to end.

## How the Text Supports the Argument

The fragment supports the argument because it states the enforcement condition directly. Law-like rules do not protect unless some power can make noncompliance costly.

## Broader Explanation

The laws of nature tell people to seek peace and keep covenants, but Hobbes does not think moral insight alone is enough. Rational agents need assurance. That assurance comes through a common power with public force.

This distinguishes Hobbes from theories that place more weight on moral motivation, civic virtue, or public reason. For Hobbes, the social contract creates peace by changing incentives under a sovereign.

## Underlying Assumptions

- Rational people fear being exploited by defectors.
- Moral rules require enforcement under conditions of distrust.
- Peace is impossible if private judgment remains the final authority.

## What This Argument Does Not Prove

It does not prove that the sovereign must be unlimited in every respect. It does not prove that coercion creates genuine moral agreement. It proves a narrower point: agreements are unstable without enforcement.

## Opposing Views

- **Contractualism:** Public justifiability may ground obligation without reducing it to fear of punishment.
- **Republicanism:** Civic virtue and institutions can sustain compliance without absolute sovereignty.
- **Anarchism:** Some traditions deny that coercive sovereign authority is necessary for order.
