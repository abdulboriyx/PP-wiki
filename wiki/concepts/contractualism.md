# Concept: Contractualism

**Type:** Moral and political theory
**Strand:** Kantian social contract tradition

---

## Definition

Contractualism holds that moral norms are those that could be justified to each person as a rational agent — that is, principles no one could reasonably reject. The primary motivation is not self-interest but a commitment to publicly justify the standards of morality to which each will be held. Rationality requires respecting persons, and respecting persons requires justifiability.

Contractualism is contrasted with [contractarianism](contractarianism.md), which derives moral norms from rational self-interest rather than from the requirement of justifiability to others.

---

## Core Claim

A moral principle is valid if and only if no one could reasonably reject it. "Reasonable rejection" is not the same as self-interested rejection: a principle can be advantageous to me and still be reasonably rejectable; a principle can be disadvantageous to me and yet not be reasonably rejectable.

---

## Interpretations

### Rawls
The *original position* behind a *veil of ignorance* (not knowing one's social position, natural talents, or conception of the good) generates principles of justice that rational agents would choose. This device ensures fairness and impartiality without appealing to prior moral norms extrinsic to the procedure. Rawls is the paradigm contemporary contractualist.

### Scanlon
*What We Owe to Each Other* (1998): moral wrongness = acting on principles that others could reasonably reject. The relevant standards are not self-interest but what each person, as a person seeking agreement on terms of mutual recognition, could accept.

---

## Distinction from Contractarianism

| Dimension | Contractarianism | Contractualism |
|---|---|---|
| Motivation of parties | Self-interest (non-tuistic) | Commitment to justifiability |
| Standard for agreement | Rational advantage | Reasonable non-rejectability |
| Key tradition | Hobbesian | Kantian |
| Paradigm thinkers | Gauthier, Narveson, Buchanan | Rawls, Scanlon |
| Grounds for inclusion | Reciprocity / capacity to contribute | Moral equality / personhood |

---

## Significance for Inclusion

Because contractualism grounds moral standing in personhood rather than reciprocal contribution, it is better positioned to include the severely disabled, animals (indirectly), and others who cannot contribute to social cooperation. A contractualist can argue that excluding such individuals is a principle that could be reasonably rejected by them (or on their behalf).
