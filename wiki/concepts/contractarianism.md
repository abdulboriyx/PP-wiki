# Concept: Contractarianism

**Type:** Political and moral theory
**Strand:** Hobbesian social contract tradition

---

## Definition

Contractarianism holds that moral norms and the legitimacy of political authority derive from a contract or mutual agreement among self-interested rational agents. The key motivation is not pre-existing moral duty to others but rational self-interest: individuals cooperate because they are (a) vulnerable to the depredations of others and (b) able to benefit from cooperation.

Contractarianism is distinguished from [contractualism](contractualism.md), which stems from the Kantian tradition and grounds morality in what principles could be publicly justified to each person as a rational agent — not merely what it is rational for self-interested parties to agree to.

---

## Core Elements

### 1. Initial Situation
Every contractarian theory specifies a baseline — the situation that obtains if no agreement is reached. Called variously:
- **State of nature** (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau)
- **Initial bargaining position** (Gauthier)
- **Original position** (Rawls — though Rawls is better classified as contractualist)

The initial situation sets the "no-agreement point" in bargaining. Its hostility or mildness shapes what terms of contract are rational to accept.

### 2. Characterization of Parties
Contractors are assumed to be:
- **Non-tuistic**: primarily self-interested, without built-in preferences for others' well-being
- **Instrumentally rational**: rational action = maximizing satisfaction of one's own subjective preferences
- Capable of cooperation: they can produce more together than alone

### 3. Derivation of Moral Norms
Moral norms are whatever rational self-interested agents in a fair initial situation would agree to. There is no appeal to prior, independent moral facts. (This is what distinguishes contractarianism from contractualism.)

## Interpretations

### Hobbes
The state of nature is a war of all against all: life is "solitary, poore, nasty, brutish, and short." Rational agents alienate their rights to a sovereign (*alienation contract*) to escape this. Result: can justify strong, even authoritarian, government.

### Gauthier
*Morals by Agreement* (1986): agents are constrained maximizers who adopt dispositions to cooperate when among like-minded cooperators. Agreement principle: **minimax relative concession** — minimize the maximum relative concession any party makes. Later revised (2013) toward **maximin proportionate gain**, treating parties as Pareto-optimizers rather than bargaining maximizers. Initial position conditioned by a modified **Lockean Proviso**: no one may have bettered themselves by worsening others.

### Narveson
Libertarian contractarianism: primary motivation is fear of others' depredations, yielding minimal government and near-absolute property rights.

## Key Controversies

| Issue | Dispute |
|---|---|
| Historical vs. hypothetical contract | Hume: ancestors' consent cannot bind us. Dworkin: a hypothetical contract is not a contract at all. Gauthier: the contract is a heuristic for practical rationality, not a binding historical fact. |
| Compliance problem | Hampton's dilemma: if cooperation failures stem from passion, parties remain passionate after the contract; if from rationality (prisoner's dilemma), defection after the contract is equally rational. |
| Fairness without prior norms | How can the initial situation be fair/impartial without appealing to moral norms that precede the contract? (Thrasher 2014 critique; Thoma 2015 defense.) |
| Constrained vs. straightforward maximization | Gauthier's move to "constrained maximization" has been called moralization of practical reason, departing from strict contractarianism (Moehler 2020). |

---

## Critiques

- **Normativity objection** (Southwood): contractarian morality gives agents no reason for guilt or remorse — only self-directed disappointment at irrationality.
- **Impartiality objection**: powerless persons cannot be rationality-required to be treated equally, since it is not mutually advantageous to include them.
- **Racial and gender critiques** (Williams 1991; Kittay 1999): contract requires independent rational agents; historically, Black people and women were classified as incapable of contract, excluding them from justice.
- **Inclusion problem**: the severely disabled, animals, and the global poor may fall outside the scope of justice if they cannot reciprocate. See [Debate: Inclusion and Exclusion](../debates/inclusion-exclusion.md).
