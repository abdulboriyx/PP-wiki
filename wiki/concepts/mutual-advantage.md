# Concept: Mutual Advantage

**Type:** Normative concept; core justificatory principle in contractarianism

---

## Definition

Mutual advantage is the condition in which social cooperation produces benefits for each participant that exceed what each could achieve acting alone. It is the primary contractarian justification for accepting moral constraints on self-interest: it is rational to be moral because being moral enables mutually advantageous cooperation.

---

## Role in Contractarianism

In the Hobbesian tradition, two facts about human beings ground the motivation to contract:
1. We are vulnerable to the depredations of others.
2. We can all benefit from cooperation with others.

Mutual advantage addresses both: it makes cooperation individually rational. Without moral constraints, cooperation collapses into a prisoner's dilemma where everyone defects and everyone is worse off. Morality as mutual advantage solves this coordination problem.

---

## The Circumstances of Justice

Rawls (following Hume) argued that mutual advantage requires specific conditions:
- **Moderate scarcity**: If resources were unlimited, cooperation would be unnecessary; if impossible, it would be pointless.
- **Rough equality**: If one party could dominate all others, they would have no need for agreement.
- **Some convergent interest**: Parties must have overlapping interests for cooperation to be beneficial.

These conditions define the *circumstances of justice* — the conditions under which justice is both possible and necessary.

---

## Tension: Mutual Advantage vs. Moral Equality

The mutual-advantage framework has a structural tension with egalitarian intuitions:

- **Claim**: It is rational to include only those who can both benefit from and reciprocate to social cooperation.
- **Implication**: Those who cannot reciprocate (severely disabled persons, animals, the global poor relative to wealthy contractors) may fall outside the scope of justice.
- **Objection** (Kittay 1999; Nussbaum 2006): This produces morally unacceptable exclusions.

See [Debate: Inclusion and Exclusion](../debates/inclusion-exclusion.md).

---

## Mutual Advantage and Exploitation

Gauthier argues that contractarianism should protect against exploitation:
- Moral norms derived from mutual advantage *disapprove* of relationships that are not mutually beneficial, even when affected parties consent under conditions of adaptive preference.
- This has been used to support feminist arguments about the exploitative nature of care relationships structured by sexist norms (Hampton 1993).

Dimock (1999) warns, however, that allowing positive *tuistic* preferences (concern for others' well-being) in contractarian bargaining can create a different form of exploitation: individuals (particularly women) who genuinely care about others may be systematically disadvantaged in negotiations.
