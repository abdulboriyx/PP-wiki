# Concept: Justice

## Definition

Justice is a central concept in political philosophy, concerning the fair and proper administration of laws, the distribution of wealth and resources, and the moral righteousness of individuals and societies.

## Platonic Interpretation

In Plato's *[The Republic](../sources/plato-republic.md)*, justice (dikaiosyne) is defined not merely in terms of external actions, but as an internal state of harmony. 

- **In the State:** Justice is the structural harmony of the city where each of the three classes (rulers, auxiliaries, producers) performs its own specific function and does not interfere with the others.
- **In the Individual:** Justice is the corresponding harmony of the [Tripartite Soul](../concepts/tripartite-soul.md), where reason rules, spirit assists, and appetites are kept in check.

This interpretation is developed through the [Kallipolis](kallipolis.md). Socrates looks for justice in the city because it is easier to see at a larger scale, then applies the same structure to the soul.

## Claim / Interpretation / Evidence

- **Claim:** Justice is psychic and civic order.
- **Interpretation:** Plato treats injustice as internal disorder, not merely rule-breaking.
- **Evidence:** Book IV links the city's three classes with the soul's three parts and defines justice as each part doing its own work.

## Opposing Interpretations

- **Thrasymachus:** Argues that justice is nothing but "the advantage of the stronger" (might makes right). See [Thrasymachus on Justice](../debates/thrasymachus-justice.md).
- **Contractarianism:** Views justice as arising from mutual agreement among self-interested rational individuals (e.g., Hobbes, Gauthier). See [Contractarianism](contractarianism.md).
- **Contractualism:** Views justice in terms of principles that no one could reasonably reject (e.g., Rawls, Kant). See [Contractualism](contractualism.md).

## Tensions

Platonic justice preserves order by assigning unequal functions. Modern contractualist and democratic views often begin from equal moral or political standing, creating a direct disagreement over whether hierarchy can itself be just.
