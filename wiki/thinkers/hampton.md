# Thinker: Jean Hampton (1954–1996)

**Tradition:** Analytic political philosophy; social contract theory; feminist philosophy

---

## Core Ideas

Jean Hampton was a major critical voice on contractarianism, known for both her reconstructive work on Hobbes and her critiques of Gauthier.

### 1. Hampton's Dilemma (Re: Hobbes)
In *Hobbes and the Social Contract Tradition* (1986), Hampton argues Hobbes faces a structural dilemma:

- **Passion account**: If the state of nature is a war because of passions (greed, fear), those passions persist after the contract — compliance is not secured.
- **Rationality account** (prisoner's dilemma): If failure to cooperate is the rational outcome, the same reasoning applies after the contract — defection remains rational.

This dilemma has direct relevance to contemporary contractarians (including Gauthier) who rely on a sub-optimal non-cooperative baseline to motivate agreement.

### 2. Interaction as More Than Instrumentally Valuable
Hampton argues that if social interaction were purely instrumentally valuable (valuable only for the fruits of cooperation), it would be unlikely that self-interested cooperators could solve the compliance problem without some natural inclination toward morality. Genuine sociality — caring about others for their own sake — may be required.

### 3. Agreement with Gauthier on Exploitation
Hampton agrees with Gauthier that contractarianism is right to require moral norms to *limit* self-sacrifice: no individual should be required to cooperate in ways that are net costly to them. This provides contractarianism with anti-exploitation implications that Hampton supports.

### 4. Feminist Contractarianism
Hampton (1993) defends a feminist version of contractarianism: mutual advantage contractarianism can underwrite feminist claims about exploitative intimate relationships without presupposing the intrinsic value of persons (the contractualist move).
