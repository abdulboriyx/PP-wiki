# Thinker: Charles W. Mills (1951–2021)

**Tradition:** Critical philosophy of race; political philosophy; subversive contractarianism

---

## Core Ideas

Mills uses contractarian analysis to expose and condemn white supremacy as an implicit political contract rather than a deviation from liberal values.

### 1. The Racial Contract (*The Racial Contract*, 1997)
White people have had an actual, historical — sometimes explicit, often only implicit — contract to enforce white supremacy. This contract has moral, political, and epistemological dimensions:

- *Moral terms*: White persons evaluate the lives of white people more highly than non-white people.
- *Political terms*: Non-white people are deprived of effective political power.
- *Epistemological terms*: Whites are positioned as intellectually superior; non-white perspectives are systematically discounted (*epistemology of ignorance*).

The racial contract is not metaphorical: it has been enforced through law, custom, and violence. Its existence is historically documented.

### 2. The Domination Contract (later work)
Mills extended his analysis in *Contract and Domination* (with Pateman, 2007) to include a more general "domination contract" encompassing race, gender, and class. He also applies the framework normatively to the issue of reparations for slavery.

### 3. Salvaging Contract Theory
Unlike Pateman, Mills holds that contract theory can be *reformed*. If we begin from a realistic, non-ideal starting point — acknowledging existing injustice — and ask how it ought rationally to be eliminated, contract theory can serve emancipatory purposes.

**Note**: Mills's positive theory is contractualist (grounded in moral equality) rather than contractarian.

### 4. Non-Ideal Theory
Mills was a major advocate for non-ideal theory in political philosophy: rather than asking what perfectly just institutions would look like, ask what steps toward justice are required from conditions of pervasive injustice.
