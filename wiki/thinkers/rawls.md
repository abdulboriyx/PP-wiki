# Thinker: John Rawls (1921–2002)

**Tradition:** Liberal political philosophy; contractualism; justice theory

---

## Core Ideas

1. **Contractualist revival**: Rawls effectively resurrected social contract theory in the second half of the 20th century. His approach is contractualist (Kantian) rather than contractarian (Hobbesian): parties are motivated by a commitment to fair terms of cooperation, not mere self-interest.

2. **Original position and veil of ignorance**: Principles of justice are those that rational agents would choose from an *original position* behind a *veil of ignorance* — without knowledge of their social position, natural talents, conception of the good, or the society's economic or political situation. This device ensures impartiality.

3. **Two principles of justice**:
   - *Equal basic liberties*: Each person has an equal right to the most extensive system of basic liberties compatible with a like system for all.
   - *Difference principle*: Social and economic inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society.

4. **Circumstances of justice** (following Hume): Justice is possible and necessary only under conditions of moderate scarcity and rough equality of power — the *circumstances of justice*.

5. **Threat advantage rejected**: Rawls explicitly criticizes the contractarian outcome of "to each according to his threat advantage" — outcomes that merely reflect bargaining power rather than fairness.

6. **Classification**: Rawls is typically classified as a contractualist, not a contractarian, because the original position does not model self-interested bargaining but rather fair deliberation under ignorance.

## Rawls vs. Gauthier

| Dimension | Rawls | Gauthier |
|---|---|---|
| Motivation | Commitment to fair cooperation | Rational self-interest |
| Information | Veil of ignorance | Full information |
| Agreement principle | Maximin (benefit the worst-off) | Minimax relative concession |
| Tradition | Contractualist | Contractarian |
