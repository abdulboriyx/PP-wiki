# Thinker: Carole Pateman (b. 1940)

**Tradition:** Feminist political philosophy; critical theory; subversive contractarianism

---

## Core Ideas

Pateman uses contractarian logic against the liberal contractarian tradition itself, arguing that the social contract has been an instrument of domination rather than liberation.

### 1. The Sexual Contract (*The Sexual Contract*, 1989)
There has been an implicit contract among men to enforce patriarchy. Pateman calls her approach a *conjectural history* — not a claim about a specific historical event, but an illumination of the actual history of patriarchal oppression and the ideology of liberal contract theory.

**Terms of the sexual contract**:
- *Moral*: Men evaluate women's lives as less valuable.
- *Political*: Women are deprived of effective political power.
- *Epistemological*: Men view themselves as intellectually superior to women.

The public/private split in liberal theory conceals how the "private" sphere of the family is itself governed by a contract on male dominance.

### 2. Settler Contract (later work)
Extended analysis to colonial appropriation of indigenous peoples' lands: a "settler contract" among colonizers to appropriate native lands.

### 3. Contract and Domination (*Contract and Domination*, co-authored with Charles W. Mills, 2007)
Pateman and Mills collaborate to compare the sexual and racial contracts, exploring similarities and divergences. Pateman extends her critique to settler colonialism; she holds that contractarianism is *essentially* permissive of domination.

**Disagreement with Mills**: While Pateman holds contractarianism is structurally flawed and cannot be salvaged, Mills argues contract theory can be reformed by starting from a more realistic, non-ideal baseline. (Mills's positive theory is contractualist, however.)

### 4. Adaptive Preferences
Some philosophers inspired by Pateman use social contract theory to identify and combat *adaptive preferences* — preferences formed under conditions of oppression that appear voluntary but are not genuinely free.
