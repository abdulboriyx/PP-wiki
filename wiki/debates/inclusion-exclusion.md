# Debate: Inclusion and Exclusion in the Social Contract

---

## The Problem

Social contract theories face a structural challenge: who counts as a legitimate party to the contract? The answer has direct implications for who is owed duties of justice.

In normative contractarianism, it is rational to include only those who can both **benefit from** and **reciprocate benefits to** social cooperation. This excludes — at least potentially — the severely disabled, animals, the global poor, and historically, women and non-white persons.

---

## Positions

### Position 1: Strict Mutual Advantage (Contractarian)

**Claim**: Justice requires mutual advantage. Those who cannot contribute to the cooperative surplus cannot rationally be included in the original contract.

**Arguments**:
- The circumstances of justice (Rawls/Hume): justice is possible and necessary only where cooperation is both possible and beneficial.
- Threatening parties: those who cannot destabilize society have no bargaining leverage and thus cannot negotiate inclusion.

**Implications**: The severely disabled, animals, the global poor (in global-scale contracts), and historically powerless groups fall outside the contract's scope.

**Proponents**: Strict Hobbesian contractarians; Gauthier (partly).

---

### Position 2: Inclusive Contractarianism — Strategic Inclusion (Becker 2005)

**Claim**: In practice, including most disabled persons and all caregivers is mutually advantageous.

**Arguments**:
- Most disabled persons can contribute, or could do so with accommodation or rehabilitation — and the contractarian has reason to provide such accommodation.
- A social insurance scheme covering all who need care is mutually advantageous to healthy contractors, since each faces some probability of disability.
- Trust-building: Disabled persons who rely on caregivers and yet remain forward-looking and non-hostile contribute to a climate of trust that reduces enforcement costs for all (Francis & Silvers 2005).

**Objection**: For "outliers" — those whose needs exceed any possible future contribution — the insurance scheme does not clearly work. They remain structurally excluded.

---

### Position 3: Contractualist Inclusion (Rawls, Scanlon)

**Claim**: Moral standing is grounded in personhood (rational agency), not reciprocal contribution. Any principle that excludes some persons from moral consideration is one those persons could reasonably reject.

**Arguments**:
- Excluding the disabled or non-white or women from justice is a principle those persons could reasonably reject — therefore it is morally wrong regardless of mutual advantage.
- The original position behind the veil of ignorance generates inclusion automatically: since you do not know whether you will be disabled, you would not choose principles that exclude the disabled.

**Objection from contractarians**: This assumes prior moral equality — the very thing contractualism is supposed to derive from agreement.

---

### Position 4: Racial and Gender Critique (Williams 1991; Kittay 1999; Buchanan 1993)

**Claim**: Contractarianism has historically defined the category of "rational agent capable of contract" in exclusionary ways.

**Arguments**:
- Contract requires independent agents who make and carry out promises. Historically, Black people and women were classified as *anti-will*: dependent, irrational, incapable of contract.
- By defining some as contractors and others as incapable, whole classes are excluded from the realm of justice.
- Caretakers (disproportionately women) whose interests are intertwined with those of dependents are systematically undervalued even in fair bargaining (Kittay 1999).

---

### Position 5: Animals

**Claim**: Animals fall outside the contract because they cannot engage in rational interaction or reciprocate benefits.

**Indirect-inclusion response** (Cohen 2007, 2009): A contractor may agree to the contract *conditional on* direct moral standing being granted to beings they care about (e.g., their pets). This bilateral mechanism could extend justice to animals.

**Objection**: This makes animal standing contingent on the tuistic preferences of contractors — an unstable basis that could protect some animals but not others, and only when doing so does not conflict with contractors' own interests.

---

## Core Tension

The debate exposes a fundamental tension:
- Contractarianism's strength is deriving morality from self-interest without presupposing prior moral facts.
- Its weakness is that self-interest is an unreliable guide to who deserves moral consideration — power and the ability to threaten are morally arbitrary features.
- Contractualism avoids this by presupposing moral equality, but is then accused of circular reasoning.

Neither position has fully resolved the exclusion problem.
