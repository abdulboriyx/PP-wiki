# Debate: Subversive Contractarianism

---

## Overview

"Subversive contractarianism" uses the contractarian framework not to *justify* political authority but to *expose and condemn* historical forms of domination. The key move: contractarian logic applies not only to the formation of legitimate social orders but also to the formation of oppressive ones. The social contract, read subversively, is a compact among the dominant to exploit the dominated.

This tradition does not face the Humean objection (that ancestors' consent cannot bind us) because it does not use the contract as a justification — it uses it as an explanation and indictment.

---

## Positions

### Carole Pateman — The Sexual Contract (*The Sexual Contract*, 1989)

**Claim**: There is an implicit (and sometimes explicit) contract among men to enforce patriarchy. The liberal "social contract" that supposedly creates civil society simultaneously creates a *sexual contract* that structures relations of male domination in the private sphere.

**Method**: Conjectural history — an illumination of actual patriarchal history and liberal ideology, not a claim about a single historical event.

**Terms of the sexual contract**:
- *Moral*: Women's lives are evaluated as less valuable.
- *Political*: Women are excluded from effective political power.
- *Epistemological*: Men are constituted as intellectually superior; women's knowledge and testimony are systematically discounted.

**Implication for contract theory**: Pateman argues that contractarianism is *structurally* permissive of domination — the in-group/out-group logic of contract always risks being weaponized to exclude and exploit. She does not think the theory can be salvaged.

---

### Charles W. Mills — The Racial Contract (*The Racial Contract*, 1997)

**Claim**: White people have had an actual, historical — sometimes explicit, often implicit — contract to enforce white supremacy. This is not a metaphor. It has been embodied in law, policy, ideology, and violence.

**Terms of the racial contract**:
- *Moral*: White lives are valued more highly than non-white lives.
- *Political*: Non-white people are excluded from effective political power.
- *Epistemological*: An "epistemology of ignorance" systematically distorts white cognition — whites are positioned as intellectually superior and are required by the contract to *misunderstand* race relations.

**Extension** (*Contract and Domination*, with Pateman, 2007): Mills extends the analysis to a general "domination contract" covering race, gender, and class. He uses it normatively to argue for reparations for slavery.

**Divergence from Pateman**: Mills holds that contract theory can be *reformed* — beginning from a non-ideal, realistic acknowledgment of existing injustice, contract theory can support emancipatory politics. (His positive theory is contractualist, grounded in moral equality.)

---

### Point of Convergence

Both Pateman and Mills:
- Use contractarian in-group/out-group logic to explain oppression
- Argue that the standard social contract theory's "original position" or "initial bargaining position" is contaminated by these prior domination contracts
- Treat the contract as partly historical, not purely hypothetical

---

### Point of Divergence

| | Pateman | Mills |
|---|---|---|
| Can contract theory be salvaged? | No — domination is structural | Yes — with non-ideal starting point |
| Positive theory | Skeptical of contract | Contractualist (moral equality) |
| Primary focus | Patriarchy / gender | White supremacy / race |

---

## Implication for Normative Contractarianism

If the racial and sexual contracts were *rational* (i.e., mutually advantageous for the dominant group), they constitute a *prima facie critique* of normative contractarianism: they would justify racism and sexism.

**Contractarian response** (Gauthier): Normative contractarianism disapproves of relationships not mutually advantageous to *all parties*. Non-whites and women can both benefit and reciprocate benefits — so their inclusion is rationally required, and exclusion is irrational.

**Counter-response**: This response works only if non-whites and women are treated as full parties from the start. Historically, they were not. And the Lockean Proviso, if applied rigorously, would condemn virtually all existing property arrangements as built on coerced baselines.
