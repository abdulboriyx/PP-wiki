# Debate: Thrasymachus on Justice

## The Positions

This debate is centrally featured in Book I of [Plato's](../thinkers/plato.md) *[The Republic](../sources/plato-republic.md)*.

### Thrasymachus: Justice as the Advantage of the Stronger
- **Claim:** Justice is nothing more than what is advantageous to the stronger (the ruling class). Laws are made by those in power to serve their own interests. 
- **Argument:** In every regime, the rulers decree what is "just" to benefit themselves, and punish those who transgress as "unjust." Furthermore, perfect injustice (tyranny) is more profitable and brings more happiness than perfect justice, because the unjust man gains power and wealth at the expense of the just man.
- **Interpretation:** This is an early form of moral skepticism, political realism, or legal positivism.
- **Evidence:** Book I ties justice to ruling power and presents injustice as more profitable when practiced on a large scale.

### Socrates: Justice as a Virtue and Harmony
- **Claim:** Justice is an objective virtue of the soul, and the just life is happier and more profitable than the unjust life.
- **Argument:** Socrates argues that every craft (including ruling) aims at the advantage of its subjects, not its practitioners. An unjust soul is like a diseased body—plagued by internal conflict and incapable of true happiness. Justice is a state of harmony ([Tripartite Soul](../concepts/tripartite-soul.md)) that allows an individual (or state) to function excellently.
- **Evidence:** The full answer is not completed in Book I; it requires Books II-IV, where Socrates builds the [Kallipolis](../concepts/kallipolis.md) and defines justice by function and psychic order.

## Comparison

- **Nature vs. Convention:** Thrasymachus views justice as a conventional tool of oppression, while Socrates views it as an objective, natural good.
- **Self-Interest:** Thrasymachus believes rational self-interest leads to injustice. Socrates counters that true self-interest requires justice, because an unjust soul is fundamentally miserable.
- **Power vs. Order:** Thrasymachus sees law as the expression of ruling advantage; Plato sees law as legitimate only when ordered by knowledge of the good.

## Tensions

Socrates defeats parts of Thrasymachus' formulation in Book I, but Glaucon and Adeimantus revive the deeper challenge in Book II. The debate is therefore not settled by Book I alone.
