# Debate: Contractarianism vs. Contractualism

---

## The Dispute

Both contractarianism and contractualism use the idea of agreement or contract to ground morality and/or political authority. They are often grouped together — and historically were not sharply distinguished — but are now recognized as fundamentally different theories.

---

## Positions

### Contractarianism (Hobbesian)

- **Core claim**: Moral norms derive their content and force from what self-interested rational agents would agree to in order to achieve mutual advantage.
- **Motivation**: Fear of the state of nature; gains from cooperation.
- **Parties**: Non-tuistic self-interested agents; instrumental rationality.
- **Why be moral?**: Because it is individually rational — disposing yourself to be moral enables beneficial cooperation.
- **Paradigm thinkers**: Hobbes, Gauthier, Narveson, Buchanan.

### Contractualism (Kantian)

- **Core claim**: Moral principles are valid if and only if no one could reasonably reject them. Rationality requires respecting persons as ends, which requires justifiability to each.
- **Motivation**: Commitment to publicly justify the standards to which each will be held; respect for rational agency.
- **Parties**: Persons as rational agents with equal moral standing.
- **Why be moral?**: Because morality is what rationality requires when you take seriously the equal standing of all persons.
- **Paradigm thinkers**: Kant, Rawls, Scanlon.

---

## Key Points of Disagreement

| Dimension | Contractarianism | Contractualism |
|---|---|---|
| Basis of moral norms | Rational self-interest + mutual advantage | Justifiability to each rational agent |
| Motivation of parties | Non-tuistic self-interest | Commitment to equal respect / justifiability |
| Scope of justice | Those who can contribute and reciprocate | All rational persons (and potentially beyond) |
| Inclusion of marginal cases | Structural difficulty including non-contributors | Better-positioned to include disabled, etc. |
| Hypothetical contract role | Reveals requirements of practical rationality | Ensures fairness via ignorance or idealization |
| Prior moral constraints | Controversial: may circularly presuppose morality | Justified externally by respect for persons |

---

## The Normativity Objection to Contractarianism
Southwood (2010): Contractarian morality is not sufficiently other-regarding. It gives agents no reason for guilt or remorse at wrongdoing — only self-directed disappointment at irrationality. Gauthier responds via the "liberal individual" whose moral psychology is shaped by living under mutual advantage norms.

**Contractualist reply**: Contractualism does ground other-directed moral emotions — wrongdoing wrongs the other person, not merely makes one irrational.

---

## The Question of Prior Norms
Both positions face a bootstrapping problem:
- Contractarianism: To ensure a *fair* initial situation, some constraints must be imposed — but any such constraints seem to presuppose prior moral norms, undermining the claim that all norms derive from the contract (Thrasher 2014).
- Contractualism: It appeals to rationality and respect for persons as prior constraints — but this presupposes substantive moral commitments rather than deriving them from agreement.
